Explanation of Geolocation in Websites

It is a technology that identifies the physical location of devices and people based on their geographic coordinates and IP addresses. Most of the time, in order for this technology to work the device must be connected to the internet. However, an internet connection is not needed for GPS-enabled devices becasue they use satellite signals to determine locations. The step-by-step process in geolocation is as follows:
  1. Collection: device collects raw data about its location from multiple source: sending GPS signals, Wi-Fi pings, and cellular data exchanges
  2. Transmission: raw data is sent to the central server or cloud infrastructure that operates a geolocation application
  3. Triangulation: using this raw data, the device determines its location by measuring its distance from cell towers, satellites, or Wi-Fi access points
  4. Processing: algorithms process the raw data to produce accurate location data

Limitations of Geolocation

There are multiple factors that contribute to the accuracy of a device's geolocation reading. The different factors are the device type, data collection method, and surrounding envrionment. Mobile devices generally provide better geolocation results than stationary devices because they usually have built-in GPS sensors and more constant cellular/Wi-Fi connectivity. The data collection matters because each method is not the same in accurary. For example, IP geolocation is usually less accurate than network connections. Lastly, the surronding environment matters. Urban environments typically have more cell towers in the area as compared to rural envrionments, enchancing the accuracy of geolocation data.

Geolocation Privacy Issues

Geolocation data is considered sensitive information. If this information got into the wrong hands it could put someone in a dangerous position. Therefore, organizations that handle geolocation data use security software tools and methods to safeguard the information. However, misuse is not limited to outside sources. Sometimes the organizations that collect the data are the culprits. For example, in the past organizations have not been up-front about how they plan on using the data.
